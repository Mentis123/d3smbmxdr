01 — The Shift

From sprints and catalogues to dedicated teams

We've been building the AI Factory delivery model for weeks now — five capabilities, t-shirt sizes, fractional roles. It's good thinking and it got us here. But we've landed somewhere different, and we want to be transparent about why.

The old model allocates people fractionally — core roles at 50%, specialists at 25%. On paper that's efficient. In practice it means nobody is fully present on any engagement. The customer never gets a team. They get a rotating cast of partial humans, context-switching between engagements, rebuilding rapport every standup. We deliver 2 FTE of effort from 6 people and call it a squad. It isn't.

The Squad model cuts through all of that. A dedicated team. One engagement. They own it end to end.

The core idea

Stop selling a catalogue. Sell the team. The squad is how we deliver. The sprint is what the customer buys. Capability-as-a-service — literally.

This doesn't throw away the lifecycle methodology or the work on governance and architecture. It simplifies the commercial wrapper. One product: a dedicated AI squad that moves through the customer's lifecycle with them — discovery, build, governance, operate. Same methodology. Radically simpler packaging.

The simplification

The five capabilities, the t-shirt sizes, the tier progressions — they were all ways of packaging fractional delivery into something that sounded like a product. With a dedicated squad, you don't need the packaging. The team is the product. The customer buys ongoing capability, not a menu item.

What's in a Sprint — the rate-build

A Sprint = 2 weeks / 10 working days of squad capacity. The customer is billed for two roles inside the Sprint: the Principal (Mentor) at ~half-time, and the Senior (Lead) at full-time. The two Shadows on the squad work the engagement — they're not on the customer's invoice.

Why $35k is the load-bearing number

$35,000 AUD ≈ $25,000 USD — the smallest Microsoft co-funded engagement bucket. The S tier is sized to slot inside MS partner funding. If the customer is eligible for MS funding, their first Sprint with us may not come out of their budget — that's the wedge into a key account. Funding eligibility is determined per-customer; sellers never promise it at offer time, only frame the conditional opportunity.

What the customer gets vs what they pay for

The squad on the engagement is the full 1-1-2 cell: Principal, Senior, two Shadows. The customer is billed for ~1.5 FTE (Principal half-time + Senior full-time). The Shadows do real work on the customer's problem and are absorbed as practice investment in the talent pipeline. The customer gets a four-person team and pays for one-and-a-half FTE. The practice grows tomorrow's Leads on live customer engagements without an external hiring spend.

The land-and-expand mechanic

S funded by Microsoft gets us into the account. M and L are the expand path — the discount is the incentive to commit drawdown rather than buy Sprint-by-Sprint. Top-down (BA-owned annual programs) and bottom-up (Packaged Sprints into individual workloads) reach the same accounts from two directions. Massive piece of the selling-this puzzle.

The upside: selling the team, not the rate card

Once a 1-1-2 squad has run 2–3 engagement cycles and proven itself as a high-performing unit, we stop selling individual rate cards and sell the team at a blended rate. The customer sees a senior-calibre squad with clear role accountability across Mentor, Lead, and Shadows.

06 — The Growth Model

Self-funding, demand-led

This model doesn't need investment. The squad is sourced from existing resources. It grows as demand grows. Each new squad is a temporary cost bump that resolves as engagements fill — and each cycle is faster than the last because the talent pipeline is producing.

Phase 1 — Stand Up

Squad 1 from existing bench

Redeploy existing staff into a dedicated formation. No new hires. The only cost is opportunity — moving people from fractional work into a committed squad. Initially underutilised as demand fills. Breaks even by sprint 8–9. Profitable by end of the second quarter.

Phase 2 — Scale

Squad 2 carved from Squad 1's graduates

Demand signals a second squad. Carve out resources — temporary GP dip. The Principal from Squad 1 mentors across both. Squad 1's best juniors step up as leads in Squad 2. No external hire needed for the senior roles — the Cell model produced them.

Phase 3+ — Compound

Each new squad is cheaper to stand up than the last

The talent pipeline is producing. The Principal is shared across 2–3 squads. Graduates fill lead positions organically. The practice stops competing for senior AI talent on the open market and starts growing it internally, on live customer engagements.

The pitch to leadership

No capital ask. No hiring ahead of demand. The model funds itself from existing resources and grows in lockstep with the pipeline. Every engagement we run creates future leaders for the practice — not just outcomes for the customer.

08 — Our Position

Why we're on board

We came into this building a five-capability catalogue with t-shirt sizes, tier progressions, and fractional allocation models. That work was necessary — it mapped the landscape and stress-tested the commercial options. But the more we sat with it, the more we could feel the complexity. We were packaging complexity, not eliminating it.

The squad model eliminates it. One product. One team. Full ownership. The customer doesn't choose from a menu — they get a dedicated squad that moves through their lifecycle with them. Discovery, architecture, build, governance, operate. Same people. Growing together.

The Cell model is what makes it sustainable. Without the mentorship structure, dedicated squads would just be an expensive way to lock up senior resources. With it, every engagement is simultaneously a delivery motion and a training program. The juniors do real work on real customer problems under real accountability — and they graduate into the next squad's leadership. The practice scales from within.

The economics work at rack rate from day one — 29% GP floor, no new investment, demand-led growth. The economics get better over time as squads prove themselves and we shift from rate cards to team-based pricing. And the model gives leadership something the sprint-based approach never could: a clean, fundable growth story that doesn't require a hiring plan or a capital ask.

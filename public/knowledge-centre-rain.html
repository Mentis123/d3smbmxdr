PILLAR · v0.3 DRAFT

It’s the Win97 moment for AI — and the calendar is writing our plan for us

By Scott Gosling · Microsoft Practice Manager · Data#3

The five beats of the article

  1. The moment isn’t the tech — it’s the operating reality. Win10 EOL, CPS 230, Agent 365 GA, FY27 partner motion: the calendar is writing our plan. AI stops being a project and becomes an operating environment that needs adoption, governance, and ongoing operate.
  2. License → value, across the stack. Not a single SKU play. The pivot spans Copilot, Studio, Fabric, and Foundry — and Microsoft’s FY26 partner motion has flipped to match: sell the advice and the delivery, let the license follow.
  3. The bottleneck isn’t the model. It’s buying AI like a 2018 ERP project. Fixed-scope, nine-month SOWs can’t hold a moving frontier. Customers need to be reconditioned away from waterfall and toward iteration.
  4. Capability + capacity, by the sprint. A squad. Ten-day sprints. One outcome per sprint. Same team, every sprint, until you’re live — then graduate into rolling managed service.
  5. Packaged Agentic Sprints — drawdown, not SOW. Customers buy a pack of sprints (S/M/L), draw down as work emerges. Capacity commitment, not scope commitment — exactly the shape AI work actually has. The entry tier lands inside Microsoft’s smallest funded envelope, so a customer’s first sprint with us doesn’t come out of their budget. That’s the wedge; the larger packs are how the engagement grows.

04 · The KB layer · always-on

20 questions. Always-on answers.

The blog rain promotes. The KB articles convert. Short, evergreen, brand-voice, ~200 words each. Sourced from what customers actually Google. Every blog post links to three KB articles. Sales links to them in every email.

Squad Model

What is an AI Squad?

~250w · Planned

A four-person dedicated team that delivers AI work for one customer at a time. One Mentor, one Lead, two Shadows. Same faces every sprint. Owns the outcome end-to-end.

Squad Model

What is a sprint and what does it deliver?

~220w · Planned

Ten working days. Same four people. One defined outcome. Fixed sprint price. Demo on Day 9. Retro on Day 10. Repeatable until the work is done.

Squad Model

What is a 1·1·2 squad?

~210w · Planned

One Principal mentoring across two squads (25%). One full-time Lead driving the engagement. Two full-time Shadows building. The minimum viable AI delivery shape.

Squad Model

Mentor vs Lead vs Shadow — who does what?

~280w · Planned

Mentor governs quality and risk. Lead drives the engagement and is the customer’s daily contact. Shadows build, learn the patterns, graduate up.

Squad Model

Lean Sprint vs Scaled Sprint — which do I need?

~250w · Planned

Lean is one sprint — an explore. Scaled is 3–6 sprints — a build. Most engagements start lean, then graduate to scaled when value is proven.

Squad Model

How does the squad model differ from staff augmentation?

~260w · Planned

Staff aug rents you fractions of consultants. The squad sells you a whole team that owns an outcome. You buy sprints, not hours. You track delivery, not timesheets.

Squad Model

What is a Factory Pod and when do I need one?

~240w · Planned

A specific delivery pattern within the squad model. 6–12 weeks. 1–8 use cases. Use it when you need scaled, repeatable agent build — not exploratory work.

Buying Model

What is capability-as-a-service?

~280w · Planned

A way of buying AI delivery without scoping a project up front. You buy a team, by the sprint, with a defined outcome. Predictable price. Predictable cadence. Real outcomes.

Buying Model

How is AI work priced at Data#3?

~230w · Planned

By the sprint, not by the hour. One sprint = ten working days = a fixed price. Scope a project by counting sprints, not heads.

Buying Model

What is an AAF Pilot?

~270w · Planned

Agentic AI Foundation Pilot — Data#3 funds up to $20K of professional services on a $40K–$120K engagement. De-risks the first sprint while you prove the value.

Buying Model

What is the Azure Frontier Offer (AFO)?

~260w · Planned

Microsoft-funded AI delivery — up to US$500K of services on up to US$1M Azure consumption. Covers Fabric, Foundry, Azure DB workloads.

Buying Model

How do I de-risk our first AI engagement?

~290w · Planned

Stack the funding (AAF + AFO), start with one sprint, choose a low-stakes use case with a clear owner. If sprint one ships value, sprint two earns the right to follow.

Lifecycle

What is Envision → Transform → Operate?

~280w · Planned

Our lifecycle frame for AI engagements. Three phases. Six sub-phases. Customers can land at any maturity level — you don’t have to start at the beginning.

Lifecycle

What governance controls should I expect from day one?

~310w · Planned

Guardrails on inputs and outputs. AI-TRiSM patterns. Agent 365 observability. Purview labelling. Defender + Entra integration. Governance is a wrap, not a SKU.

Lifecycle

How is Agent 365 used at Data#3?

~250w · Planned

As the cross-seam observability spine. We onboard agents through Agent 365 from day one, so governance and lifecycle controls don’t bolt on later — they ship with the build.

Lifecycle

What is the AI Factory Readiness Assessment?

~240w · Planned

A one-week front-door engagement. Maps your environment to ETO phases. Outputs a sprint-by-sprint roadmap and a defensible CFO-grade business case.

Customer Question

Where do I start with AI?

~220w · Planned

Pick one painful, repeatable task. One owner. One agent. Ten days. If it ships value, do it again. If it doesn’t, you’ve learned cheaply. That’s where everyone starts.

Customer Question

Who owns AI internally?

~240w · Planned

A Product Owner who can sign off on outcomes. A Business SME who knows the work. An IT/Data Steward who owns access. The squad fills the rest.

Customer Question

How do I avoid agent sprawl?

~250w · Planned

Centralise agent registration through Agent 365. Govern at the platform layer, not the agent layer. Treat new agents like new applications — with onboarding, ownership, retirement.

Customer Question

What happens when the agent gives the wrong answer?

~260w · Planned

A boring agent has a defined failure mode — it knows what it doesn’t know. Human-in-the-loop on high-stakes calls. Confidence scoring. Eval datasets. Telemetry on every interaction.

06 · Two things that have to land

Two pieces of the approach matter more than they sound.

Building on Amanda’s framing, the proposal is bigger than “a post or series of posts.” A thread + an always-on KB layer is a different shape, and these two principles are why that shape is worth the extra work.

One-and-done won’t beat the noise.

Microsoft’s partners are out-publishing us 10:1 on AI. A single article gets indexed and forgotten. A spine plus an always-on KB layer gives sales a reusable library. That’s the bigger prize than any single post.

The hook is “speed-to-value with proof,” not “new model.”

It’s the bar customers themselves are setting. Every post must clear the “so what?” bar in the first paragraph. Lead with methodology and we lose. Lead with “this customer went live in ten working days and here’s what we shipped” and we win.

07 · How we’ll make it

We’re testing AI-augmented authoring on ourselves first.

Claude drafts a v0.1. The named author edits to their voice in-browser. Changes get staged. We commit when ready. The aim is faster, looser, more iterative authoring — without losing the human signature on the byline.

How each post and KB article gets made
  • DRAFTClaude drafts a v0.1 of the article. Eighty percent of the way there in an afternoon per piece.
  • VOICENamed author edits to their own voice before publish. Non-negotiable. Ghost-written posts read flat — they’ll know.
  • REVIEWInline-editable HTML. Reviewers mark up in-browser. Cuts review cycle from days to hours.
  • PUBLISHEach piece lands in data3.com Knowledge Centre under the existing /blog and /kb buckets.
  • LEARNTrack what works. Refine the pattern. Iterate the next post. The campaign is the experiment.

Six weeks in the crucible: what we’ve learned since March 4

When I wrote about the agentic AI builder six weeks ago, I called 2026 the year. Six weeks in, the thing that’s surprised me most isn’t how fast the technology has moved. It’s how much faster the customers have moved than the buying model has.

By Adam Rappaport · AI Specialist, Data#3 5 min read Rough draft · April 2026

Six weeks ago I wrote that 2026 was the year of the agentic AI builder — that the crucible was here, that the gap between “reads about it” and “ships with it” was widening fast, that you either build with AI now or you watch your competitors do it. Six weeks in, the call was right. Some parts were righter than I expected. Some parts I underestimated. This is the honest update.

The original piece made three predictions: customer pull would outpace vendor capacity, governance would become the new bottleneck, and the buying model would have to change. All three are true. They’re also moving in different directions at different speeds.

What’s accelerated

Customer pull is well ahead of vendor capacity. In just the last 30 days I’ve been on calls with three Australian enterprises asking the same thing: “we don’t want to build the capability in-house, can you deliver 100%?” That used to be a once-a-quarter conversation. Now it’s weekly.

Agent platforms are stable enough to ship boring agents now — not just demos. Six weeks ago we were still arguing about which agent runtime to bet on. That argument has cooled. Copilot Studio, Power Platform, Azure AI Foundry now stack into something you can hand to a customer with a straight face.

The pattern is being named industry-wide. NTT DATA published their Smart AI Agent™ Ecosystem with a $2B FY27 revenue target. Microsoft is running “always-on” AI marketing for the whole next financial year. Capability-as-a-service stopped being a Data#3 proposition six weeks ago and started being a category.

What’s stalled

↓ Buying model

Customers want capability-as-a-service. Their procurement systems are still configured to buy 2018 ERP-shaped projects. The legal templates haven’t caught up. The CFO templates haven’t caught up. This is now the load-bearing blocker.

↓ Internal AI talent

Companies are hiring AI engineers and watching them leave six months in. The roadmap doesn’t. Pure internal-build strategies are buckling under the velocity of the technology shift.

↓ CISO comfort

Universally cited as a concern, rarely operationalised. Most security teams haven’t seen an AI risk register that works. Governance is talked about more than it’s practised.

↓ POC graduation

MIT’s 95% pilot-failure rate is still the ceiling. The reasons haven’t changed: no sponsor, no success metric, no path from sandbox to production governance.

What’s surprised us

Three things have landed faster than I expected.

Boring is already the bar. My colleague Jack Latrobe published “Building Australia’s most boring AI” on April 27. The premise — useful, reliable, sticks around — isn’t a niche taste. It’s already the operating standard customers are asking us against. Discipline beat hype in seven weeks, not seven months.

The workforce frame has legs. The next article in the series picks up the idea of agents as a workforce you hire, onboard, supervise, and retire. It works because it’s old. We’ve managed people for fifty years. Apply the muscle to agents and most of the “agent governance” conversation gets smaller, not bigger.

Customers are funding the work. “Reference customer” is becoming a market mechanism, not a one-off case study. Vendors and integrators are funding pilots in exchange for a published win. The asymmetry that used to favour brand-name SIs is collapsing fast.

“The crucible was real. The technology held. The governance held. The buying model didn’t.”

What’s coming in the next six weeks

My colleague Scott Gosling is publishing “It’s the Windows 97 moment for AI” in the next week, picking up the buying-model problem head-on. Mark Pattie is writing about what fortnightly delivery actually looks like inside a squad. Richard Dornhart is writing about why the governance gap shows up at the operations layer, not the model layer.

Through all of it, we’re testing “AI-augmented authoring” on ourselves. This piece you’re reading was drafted by Claude against my own working notes, then edited by me in-browser, then committed. The cycle is days instead of weeks. If it works at our scale, it changes how every blog, KB article, and customer brief inside Data#3 gets made.

Twelve months minus six weeks left. Build with us.

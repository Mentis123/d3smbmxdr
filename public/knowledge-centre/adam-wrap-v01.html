Eight questions every customer asks before buying AI

After ten months of these conversations — pre-sales calls, lifecycle assessments, AAF pilots, governance workshops — the question set converges. Here it is, with an answer for each. Save the page. Share with your team.

By Adam Rappaport · AI Specialist, Data#3 7 min read Rough draft · April 2026

If you sit through enough customer conversations about AI, the same eight questions surface in nearly every one. Different industries. Different sizes. Different maturity. Same questions. This piece collects them and answers each in a paragraph — the way I’d answer it sitting across the table from a CIO at the start of an engagement. Save it. Share it. If your team’s question isn’t here, send it to me and I’ll add it.

Where do I start?

Start with a use case, not a tool. Pick one task that’s painful, repetitive, well understood, and owned by someone who can sign off on outcomes. Stand up a single agent that handles that one task. Run it for ten working days. Measure outcomes against a baseline. If it ships value, run a second one. If it doesn’t, retire it cleanly and try a different task. Don’t try to design the whole agent estate before you’ve seen what one looks like in production.

KB · “Where do I start with AI?”

Who owns this internally?

Three named roles, minimum. A Product Owner who can sign off on outcomes and prioritise use cases. A Business SME who knows how the work actually gets done today. An IT or Data Steward who owns access and security. Without those three, you don’t have an AI engagement — you have a science project. The squad delivers; your team sponsors. Both sides own the outcome.

How do I avoid agent sprawl?

Govern at the platform layer, not the agent layer. Centralise registration through Agent 365 from day one. Treat new agents like new applications — they get onboarded, owned, monitored, retired. The trap is treating agents as “just AI” outside your normal IT governance — that’s how you end up with three hundred shadow agents nobody owns. Same discipline as shadow IT, applied earlier.

What happens when the agent gives the wrong answer?

A boring agent has a defined failure mode. It knows what it doesn’t know. Confidence scoring on outputs. Human-in-the-loop on high-stakes decisions. Eval datasets. Telemetry on every interaction. The question to ask isn’t “will it ever be wrong?” — it will. The question is “what does the agent do when it’s uncertain?” If the answer is “hallucinate confidently,” you’ve bought the wrong agent.

How do we handle the right data, securely?

Ground every agent in the customer’s own data through governed channels. Microsoft Fabric, Dataverse, SharePoint with Purview labels — all of these have agent-aware integration paths. Don’t feed agents from copy-pasted CSVs. Don’t let agents reach into systems via API keys with broader scope than the human role they’re replacing. The classification, the access controls, the audit trail — all the same patterns you already use for people, applied to agents.

What’s the real budget — I keep hearing different numbers?

There’s a delivery cost and a consumption cost, and they move in opposite directions. Delivery (the team, the build) compresses with capability-as-a-service — sprint pricing is fixed, predictable, transparent. Consumption (Azure AI, Copilot Studio, Foundry) scales with usage and is usually offset by Microsoft funding programs (AFO, AAF Pilot). For a first agent in production, target $40K–$120K of engagement, partly funded, with consumption ramping over time. If someone’s quoting you a $1M–$3M number for “an AI strategy and platform,” that’s the old buying model. Walk away.

How do we keep up when the technology keeps changing?

You don’t. Not as an in-house team. Not at the pace the frontier is moving right now. The math doesn’t work — by the time you’ve hired, onboarded, and retained an AI engineer, the patterns they were hired on are six months obsolete. The realistic move is to buy capability-as-a-service from a partner whose whole job it is to track the frontier, and graduate your own people into that team over time. The ownership transfers. The frontier-tracking stays with the partner.

What does “good” look like in 90 days?

A working backlog of prioritised use cases. At least one agent in production, owned by a real human with a defined success metric. ROI numbers tied to actual deployments, not pilot vapourware. A repeatable sprint cadence for the next agent and the one after. A growing internal capability that doesn’t depend on a single hire’s brain. The sign that 90 days has gone well isn’t a strategy doc — it’s a second sprint that’s easier than the first because you’ve got the muscle.

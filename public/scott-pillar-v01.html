It’s the Windows 97 moment for AI — and the way you buy it has to change too

Adam said build. Jack said make it boring.
Here’s why you can’t buy it the old way anymore.

By Scott Gosling · Microsoft Practice Manager, Data#3 5 min read Draft · April 2026

Every twenty years or so, software stops being something you decide to use and starts being something you assume. Word processors did it in the 1980s. Browsers did it in the 1990s. Email did it just after that. The customers who handled those transitions well had one thing in common: they figured out how to buy the new thing before they figured out how to build with it.

If you grew up around computers in the late nineties, you’ll remember the Windows 95 and Windows 97 moment. Not the operating system itself — the moment when computing stopped being a project and became an operating reality. Office on every desk. The browser as the new front door. Email as the way work happened. The companies that treated it as an “IT thing” got eaten by the ones that treated it as a way of working.

We’re in the same kind of moment with AI — faster, louder, and with more vendors trying to sell you the answer before you’ve worked out the question. My colleague Adam Rappaport called it in March. The crucible is here, and you either build with AI or you fall behind the customers who do. Then last month, Jack LaTrobe followed up with the standard most projects miss: build AI that’s boring — useful, reliable, sticks around, has a real impact. Discipline over hype.

Both of those pieces are about the work. This one is about the contract.

The 82% / 95% paradox

Two numbers explain why most AI initiatives stall.

82%
of business leaders say they expect AI agents to be operating in their organisation within 12 to 18 months.
Microsoft Work Trend Index 2025
95%
of generative AI pilots fail to make it into production with measurable business value.
MIT NANDA, 2024

Both are true at the same time. Demand is real. Failure is structural. The space between those two numbers is where every CIO in Australia is currently standing — trying to work out how to be in the 82% without being in the 95%.

The bottleneck isn’t the model

Most failed AI projects we’ve seen at Data#3 didn’t fail because the models weren’t good enough. Or because the data wasn’t ready. Or because the change management didn’t land. Sometimes those things were factors. But the load-bearing problem — the one that quietly takes down most of the others — is much more boring.

People are still buying AI like it’s a 2018 ERP project.

A scope. A statement of work. A fixed roster of fractional resources. A nine-month plan with a kick-off and a go-live. The trouble is that the technology you’re buying that way isn’t a fixed thing — it’s a moving frontier. Six weeks into the plan, the model you scoped on has been replaced by a better one. The agent platform you specified shipped a feature that obsoletes a third of your build. The integration partner you booked has rotated their senior engineer onto another engagement.

“The buying model assumes the technology is the constant and the team is the variable. With AI, it’s the other way around.”

When the technology moves faster than the contract, you don’t need a better contract — you need a different shape of contract. One that assumes change instead of resisting it. One that gives you the team for as long as the work takes, not for as long as the SOW says.

What we’re doing about it

At Data#3 we’ve been quietly rebuilding our AI delivery model around something we’re calling capability-as-a-service. The unit isn’t a project. The unit is a squad — a four-person dedicated team with one mentor, one lead, two builders. The unit of sale isn’t hours; it’s a sprint — ten working days, fixed price, one defined outcome.

If you want to deliver Jack’s boring AI, you need a boring buying model to wrap around it: predictable, repeatable, durable. The repetition is the point. Same four people, every sprint, until you’re live. Then keep them, or graduate your own people into their seats. The output isn’t a deliverable handover — it’s an in-house AI delivery muscle that compounds.

It’s not a radical idea. The Analytics practice next to mine has been running this exact pattern for a year, calling it Data Factory. Six-week engagements, three two-week sprints, customers love it, ingestion effort cut by 70%. We’re not inventing the model — we’re extending it to where AI work happens. The plumbing is already proven.

The Win97 customers won

When Office and the browser became the new operating reality, the customers who pulled ahead weren’t the ones with the cleverest IT departments. They were the ones who let the new tools change how they bought software. They moved off custom builds to suites. Off perpetual licenses to enterprise agreements. Off “rollouts” to “adoption.” The shape of the contract changed, and the value followed.

AI is the same kind of moment. The customers who’ll pull ahead aren’t the ones who picked the right model in 2025 — that model will be obsolete by FY27 anyway. They’ll be the ones who got their buying model right early. Bought the team, not the tool. Bought the sprint, not the project. Bought the capability, not the deliverable.

If you’re inside the 82% — expecting agents in production within 18 months — the question isn’t which AI to build. It’s how to buy AI delivery in a shape that lets the technology keep changing while you keep shipping. We’ve got a strong opinion on what that shape looks like. We’d like to talk to you about it.

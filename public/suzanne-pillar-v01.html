Treat your agents like a workforce

Adam said build. Jack said make it boring. Here’s the part nobody’s saying out loud: agents aren’t tools you install. They’re people you hire.

By Suzanne Maroc · Solution Sales Specialist for Business Applications, Data#3 5 min read Draft · Week 3 of the Boring AI series

When you bring someone new onto your team, you don’t just turn them loose. You write a job description. You interview against it. You give them tools, access, and a manager. You set expectations. You measure performance. Eventually — gracefully — you part ways. Most organisations have spent fifty years getting good at this. So why are we deploying AI agents like applications?

My colleagues have been writing about this from two angles already. Adam Rappaport called 2026 the year to build with AI. Jack Latrobe followed up with what good looks like — boring: useful, reliable, sticks around, has real impact. This piece is about the part of building boring AI nobody’s talking about yet.

You’re not buying software. You’re hiring a workforce.

The shift isn’t subtle

A copilot is a tool you use. An agent is a worker you supervise. The difference matters because everything that follows — governance, role design, cost, accountability — falls out of which mental model you start with.

A tool gets installed. A teammate gets onboarded. A tool gets a licence. A teammate gets a role. A tool gets uninstalled when the project ends. A teammate gets retired when their job is done.

If you’re going to put dozens of agents into your organisation in the next 18 months — and Microsoft’s data says you are — the question stops being “which AI tools should we license?” and starts being “how do we run a workforce that’s half human, half agent?”

Every agent should have a job description

In Business Applications we’ve been writing employee records for years. Every person in your D365 system has a role, a reporting line, a set of permissions, performance metrics, a lifecycle status. We don’t bristle at this for humans. It’s common sense. It’s just how organisations work.

Most agents shipped today have none of that. They’re running production work with no documented role, no defined success metric, no clear owner, no retirement plan. It’s the same problem as shadow IT — just faster.

A boring agent — one that sticks around and earns its keep — looks more like this:

Role
Case Triage Officer · classifies inbound service requests and routes to the right team
Reports to
Customer Service Lead · daily review of escalated cases
Access
Service Cloud read-only · Knowledge base · Routing rules · No customer PII at full resolution
Success metric
Routing accuracy >90% · Time to triage <45 seconds · Escalation rate within tolerance
Lifecycle
Onboarded 2026-05-01 · Probation review 2026-05-31 · Annual recontract

It’s not radical. It’s the kind of structure HR has been using since the 1970s. The interesting part isn’t that we’re inventing it for agents. It’s that we’ve been deploying agents without it.

The hiring loop, applied to agents

Every team I’ve sat with already runs a hiring loop. Apply the same five steps to your next agent and most of the “agent governance” conversations get smaller, not bigger.

  1. Write the job description.

    What outcome do you need delivered? What systems do they need to touch? What does success look like in numbers? If you can’t answer those, you don’t need an agent yet — you need a clearer business problem.

  2. Interview against it.

    Run the agent against real cases before it’s let near production. Eval datasets. Smell tests. The same instinctive “does this person actually understand the job?” check you do for human candidates.

  3. Onboard them.

    Register the agent in Agent 365. Connect it to the data sources it needs — and only those. Give it guardrails on what it’s allowed to decide alone vs what it must escalate.

  4. Probation period.

    First 90 days under direct human supervision. Performance tracked weekly. Iterate on the job description, the access, the prompts. Most agents need a probation just like most hires do.

  5. Promote, retain, or retire.

    Once the agent proves itself, expand its remit or hire another. When its role is no longer needed, retire it cleanly. Don’t leave it running because nobody remembers to turn it off — that’s how agent sprawl starts.

Governance falls out for free

Here’s the trick I didn’t expect when I started thinking this way: the moment you treat agents as a workforce, AI governance stops being a separate, scary, top-down thing and starts being a set of muscles you already have.

“Access management is a workforce muscle. Role assignment is a workforce muscle. Performance review is a workforce muscle. Retirement is a workforce muscle. We don’t need new categories — we need to apply the ones we’ve had for fifty years.”

When the CISO asks “how do we govern these agents?” the answer is “the same way we govern our people, applied to a different kind of worker.” That’s a much shorter conversation than the one most security teams are currently having.

Where to start — one role, one agent, ten days

You don’t need to design your whole agent workforce in one go. The opposite, in fact. Pick one role in your organisation that’s painful, repetitive, well-understood. Write the job description. Stand up one agent. Onboard it through Agent 365 the same way you’d onboard a new hire. Run it for a sprint — ten working days. Measure outcomes against the job description.

If it works, hire another. If it doesn’t, retire it cleanly and try a different role. The workforce grows the same way every workforce grows: one good hire at a time.

The boring part is the point

There’s a temptation, with anything new and exciting, to invent a new framework for it. Resist it. The hiring frame works because it’s old. Onboarding works because it’s old. Performance reviews work because they’re old. The boring AI Jack wrote about isn’t boring because the technology is dull — it’s boring because the management around it should feel familiar.

Build it — Adam’s call. Make it boring — Jack’s standard. Hire it like you’d hire any other teammate — that’s the bit I’d add. Spoiler: it does.
